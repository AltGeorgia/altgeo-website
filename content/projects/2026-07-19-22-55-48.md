description: Pre-Implementation PrEP Delivery Preferences for High-Risk Women
  This pre-implementation study, functioning as an administrative supplement to
  a parent grant dedicated to training Caucasus-region implementation
  scientists, addresses critical public health gaps in Georgia by exploring the
  barriers and preferred delivery methods for Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)
  among women at elevated risk of HIV. Currently, PrEP programs in Georgia
  slow-moving and primarily target men who have sex with men and transgender
  populations, leaving underserved women - such as female partners of people who
  inject drugs, female sex workers, transgender women, and women who use drugs -
  without tailored prevention access. To design a more accessible,
  women-friendly program that bypasses restrictive requirements like mandatory
  visits to centralized AIDS centers, this study targets a sample size of 540
  women across Tbilisi and Batumi. Recruitment will be conducted in partnership
  with peer workers and social workers from harm reduction and methadone
  facilities. Utilizing mixed-methods implementation strategies, the study will
  conduct nominal group technique focus groups with 40 women to gather
  qualitative data, followed by computer-assisted personal interviewing with 500
  women using a choice-based conjoint experiment. By employing Hierarchical
  Bayes procedures to systematically analyze trade-offs and calculate attribute
  relative importance scores, the research team aims to determine precise
  patient preferences regarding crucial delivery logistics - such as alternative
  testing or screening venues, adherence support methods, and dispensing
  timelines - to ultimately inform and optimize future nationwide HIV prevention
  policies.
